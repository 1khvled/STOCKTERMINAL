1 CAPM & WACC Solver

The Hurdle Rate of Capital — Why a Dollar Tomorrow Costs More Than a Dollar Today
🧠 Theoretical Concept

Every firm has a cost of capital — the minimum return it must generate to justify its existence from the perspective of those who funded it. This is not accounting cost. It is opportunity cost: what investors could have earned elsewhere at equivalent risk.

CAPM answers the equity side of that question. WACC blends equity and debt into a single discount rate used in every DCF model.

Why this matters: If a firm earns 8% on invested capital but its WACC is 10%, it is destroying value — regardless of what the income statement says. The entire logic of intrinsic valuation collapses without a rigorous WACC.

📐 Mathematical Formulas

Cost of Equity (CAPM): $$K_e = R_f + (\beta \times ERP)$$

Weighted Average Cost of Capital: $$WACC = \left(\frac{E}{V} \times K_e\right) + \left(\frac{D}{V} \times K_d \times (1 - t)\right)$$

🔢 Variable Breakdown
Variable Definition Source / Notes
$R_f$ Risk-Free Rate 10Y US Treasury yield (^TNX), updated dynamically
$\beta$ Systematic Risk (Beta) Covariance of asset returns with market; $\beta=1$ = market risk
$ERP$ Equity Risk Premium Standard academic consensus: 5.5% (Damodaran)
$K_e$ Cost of Equity Output of CAPM — minimum return equity investors demand
$K_d$ Cost of Debt Effective interest rate on outstanding debt; use pre-tax rate
$t$ Corporate Tax Rate Effective tax rate from income statement
$E/V$ Equity Weight Market cap ÷ (Market cap + Total debt)
$D/V$ Debt Weight Total debt ÷ (Market cap + Total debt)
$V$ Total Firm Value Market cap + Total debt (market values, not book)
⚠️
strong>Common Trap — Book vs. Market Values: Always use market value of equity (market cap), not book equity, when computing $E/V$ and $D/V$. Using book values systematically understates equity weight for growth companies and inflates WACC.
📊 Diagnostics & Thresholds
WACC Range Signal Interpretation
< 6% 🟢 Low Hurdle Capital-light, low-beta, investment-grade debt. Easy to clear. Common in utilities/consumer staples.
6% – 10% 🟡 Normal Range Most S&P 500 large caps. Moderate leverage. Reasonable equity risk.
10% – 14% 🟠 Elevated High beta, sub-investment grade, or capital-heavy business model.
> 14% 🔴 High Hurdle Distressed, speculative, or emerging market exposure. DCF extremely sensitive to assumptions.

Capital Structure Intuition:

  • High $E/V$ (equity-heavy): Tech firms, asset-light models. WACC dominated by $K_e$.
  • High $D/V$ (debt-heavy): Utilities, REITs, leveraged buyouts. Debt tax shield reduces WACC — until leverage becomes distress risk.
  • The tax shield ($K_d \times (1-t)$) is why debt is cheaper than equity. Interest is deductible; dividends are not.

strong>Scenario: NVDA in late 2024. $R_f = 4.3%$ (10Y Treasury). $\beta = 1.72$. $ERP = 5.5%$.

2 Multi-Stage Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Model

The Present Value of All Future Economic Reality
🧠 Theoretical Concept

A DCF model answers one question: what is this business worth in today's dollars, given all the cash it will ever generate?

The model works in three stages:

  1. Stage 1 (Years 1–5): High-growth phase — analyst projects explicit FCF with aggressive growth assumptions
  2. Stage 2 (Years 6–10): Transition phase — growth moderates as competition intensifies
  3. Terminal Value: The perpetuity beyond Year 10 — captures 60–80% of total DCF value for most firms

The discount rate (WACC) accounts for the fact that a dollar received in Year 10 is worth far less than a dollar today — both because of inflation and because of risk.

📐 Mathematical Formulas

Stage 1 + Stage 2 Present Value: $$PV = \sum_{t=1}^{5} \frac{FCF_0 \times (1 + g_{1\text{-}5})^t}{(1 + WACC)^t} + \sum_{t=6}^{10} \frac{FCF_5 \times (1 + g_{6\text{-}10})^{t-5}}{(1 + WACC)^t}$$

Terminal Value (Gordon Growth Model): $$TV = \frac{FCF_{10} \times (1 + g_{terminal})}{WACC - g_{terminal}}$$

Present Value of Terminal Value: $$PV(TV) = \frac{TV}{(1 + WACC)^{10}}$$

Intrinsic Value Per Share: $$\text{Intrinsic Value} = \frac{PV(FCF) + PV(TV) - \text{Net Debt}}{\text{Shares Outstanding}}$$

Net Debt: $$\text{Net Debt} = \text{Total Debt} - \text{Total Cash & Equivalents}$$

🔢 Variable Breakdown
Variable Definition Notes
$FCF_0$ Base Free Cash Flow LTM (Last Twelve Months) FCF = Operating Cash Flow − CapEx
$g_{1\text{-}5}$ Stage 1 Growth Rate Analyst assumption; anchored to revenue growth + margin trajectory
$g_{6\text{-}10}$ Stage 2 Growth Rate Transitional deceleration; typically 50–70% of Stage 1 rate
$g_{terminal}$ Terminal Growth Rate Hard cap: never exceed long-run nominal GDP growth (~2.5–3%)
$WACC$ Discount Rate Must exceed $g_{terminal}$ or TV formula breaks (denominator → 0)
Net Debt Debt net of cash Negative net debt = net cash position (adds to equity value)
Shares Outstanding Diluted share count Use fully diluted (including options, RSUs, convertibles)
⚠️
strong>Terminal Value Dominance: For most growth companies, PV(TV) represents 60–80% of total intrinsic value. This means your terminal growth rate assumption is the single most important number in the model. A 0.5% change in $g_{terminal}$ can move intrinsic value by 20–30%. Treat it with extreme caution — never assume terminal growth > 3%.
💡
strong>Sensitivity Table Best Practice: Always run a 2D sensitivity matrix: WACC (rows) × terminal growth rate (columns). A single-point DCF output without sensitivity analysis is not institutional-grade work.
🚨 Quantitative Breakthrough: The Forward EPS Cash Conversion Fallback
Solving the Cyclical Cash Flow Trap

The Problem — Why TTM DCF Fails on CapEx-Heavy or Cyclical Companies:

Traditional DCF uses trailing-twelve-month (TTM) Free Cash Flow as $FCF_0$. This creates a systematic failure mode:

Equation Formula
$$FCF_{TTM} = \text{Operating Cash Flow}{TTM} - \text{CapEx}{TTM}$$

When a company undergoes a massive CapEx supercycle (e.g., NVIDIA building AI infrastructure, Samsung constructing fabs, cyclical miners at trough), $CapEx_{TTM}$ balloons while future earnings potential soars. The result: FCF goes negative or near-zero, and the DCF spits out an absurd intrinsic value (e.g., $32 for NVDA when it was trading at $800+).

This is not a company problem. It is a model failure — you are punishing a company for investing in its future.

The Solution — StockerAI Forward EPS Cash Conversion Fallback:

Equation Formula
$$\textbf{If } FCF_{trailing} \leq 0 \textbf{ and } ForwardEPS > 0:$$
Equation Formula
$$FCF_{Proxy} = Forward\ EPS \times Shares\ Outstanding$$

How the Fallback Mechanism Works:

Step Action
1 Detect: $FCF_{trailing} \leq 0$ AND $Forward\ EPS > 0$
2 Set model flag: is_forward_base = True
3 Replace $FCF_0$ with $FCF_{Proxy} = ForwardEPS \times SharesOutstanding$
4 Override growth assumptions → conservative sustainable rates
5 Stage 1: $g_{1\text{-}5} = 5%$, Stage 2: $g_{6\text{-}10} = 3%$
6 Run standard multi-stage DCF on the forward-based proxy
💡
strong>Why Forward EPS? Analyst consensus forward EPS already incorporates the normalization of CapEx cycles. It reflects what the business earns at steady-state — which is exactly what the terminal value phase of a DCF represents. Using it as a proxy base bridges the gap between the current investment cycle and the long-run profitability picture.
⚠️
strong>When NOT to use the Fallback: If a company has negative forward EPS (genuine losses), the fallback should not activate. A burning cash company is not the same as a profitable company with elevated CapEx. The fallback is for profitable businesses with distorted trailing cash flows, not loss-making startups.

strong>Scenario: FY2024. NVDA's TTM FCF was compressed by massive data center build-out investment. Simple TTM DCF output: ~$32/share (absurd vs. market price of $800+).

3 3-Stage DuPont ROE Decomposition

Deconstructing Return on Equity — Profitability, Efficiency, or Just Leverage?
🧠 Theoretical Concept

Return on Equity (ROE) is the most cited profitability metric in equity analysis. It measures how many dollars of net income a firm generates per dollar of shareholders' equity.

The problem: A company can show spectacular ROE through three entirely different paths — genuine profitability, capital efficiency, or simply borrowing aggressively to amplify returns. The 3-Stage DuPont decomposition surgically separates these three sources.

Two companies with identical 25% ROE can be radically different investments. One earns it through 40% net margins (Apple). Another earns it by carrying 10x leverage (a levered retailer). The DuPont framework reveals which.

📐 Mathematical Formula
Equation Formula
$$ROE = \underbrace{\left(\frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Revenue}}\right)}{\text{Profitability}} \times \underbrace{\left(\frac{\text{Revenue}}{\text{Total Assets}}\right)}{\text{Efficiency}} \times \underbrace{\left(\frac{\text{Total Assets}}{\text{Shareholders' Equity}}\right)}_{\text{Leverage}}$$
Equation Formula
$$ROE = \text{Net Profit Margin} \times \text{Asset Turnover} \times \text{Equity Multiplier}$$
🔢 Variable Breakdown
Component Formula What It Measures High = ?
Net Profit Margin Net Income / Revenue Pricing power + cost control 🟢 Strong moat, pricing power
Asset Turnover Revenue / Total Assets How hard assets work 🟢 Capital efficiency
Equity Multiplier Total Assets / Equity Financial leverage ⚠️ Could be risk, not quality
📊 Diagnostics & Thresholds
ROE Quality Signal Notes
< 10% 🔴 Poor Below cost of equity for most firms — destroys shareholder value
10% – 15% 🟡 Adequate Average for capital-intensive industries
15% – 25% 🟢 Good Indicates genuine competitive advantage
> 25% 🟢🟢 Excellent Franchise businesses (MSFT, AAPL, MA) — but verify source
⚠️
strong>The Debt-Inflated ROE Trap: A firm with negative book equity (accumulated losses exceed paid-in capital — common in mature buyback machines like McDonald's or Boeing) will show negative or infinite ROE. This is not a quality signal. Always decompose before interpreting. If the equity multiplier is 10x+ and profit margins are thin, the ROE is a leverage mirage.
🔬 Case Diagnostic — Two 25% ROE Companies
Metric Company A (VISA) Company B (Highly Levered Retailer)
Net Profit Margin 51% 3%
Asset Turnover 0.49x 2.1x
Equity Multiplier 1.0x 4.0x
ROE 25% 25%
Verdict 🟢 Franchise quality — earned through pricing power 🔴 Leverage illusion — one recession from distress

4 Altman Z-Score

Solvency Radar — Predicting Bankruptcy Risk Within 24 Months
🧠 Theoretical Concept

Developed by Edward Altman in 1968, the Z-Score is an OLS multivariate discriminant model that combines five financial ratios into a single score predictive of corporate bankruptcy within 2 years. Originally tested on 66 manufacturing firms, it remains one of the most validated models in corporate finance.

The Z-Score does not predict if a company will fail — it predicts how far it is from the financial profile of companies that have failed historically.

📐 Mathematical Formula
Equation Formula
$$Z = 1.2A + 1.4B + 3.3C + 0.6D + 0.999E$$
🔢 Variable Breakdown
Variable Formula What It Captures Weight
$A$ Working Capital / Total Assets Short-term liquidity buffer 1.2
$B$ Retained Earnings / Total Assets Cumulative profitability over lifetime 1.4
$C$ EBIT / Total Assets Core operating productivity of assets 3.3
$D$ Market Value of Equity / Total Liabilities Market's implied leverage coverage 0.6
$E$ Revenue / Total Assets Asset turnover / sales generation 0.999
💡
strong>Variable $C$ has the highest weight (3.3x) — EBIT/Assets. Altman found operating profitability relative to asset base is the single strongest discriminator between distressed and healthy firms. A company can survive negative working capital temporarily; it cannot survive prolonged EBIT destruction.
📊 Thresholds
Z-Score Zone Signal
$Z > 2.99$ 🟢 Safe Zone Low bankruptcy risk. Financially robust.
$1.81 \leq Z \leq 2.99$ 🟡 Gray Zone Uncertain. Watch for trend deterioration.
$Z < 1.81$ 🔴 Distress Zone High bankruptcy probability within 24 months.
⚠️
strong>Original Z-Score Limitations: Altman's original model was calibrated on manufacturing firms. For non-manufacturing or financial firms, use the Z''-Score variant (different coefficients). Also note: the model uses market cap (variable $D$), so a company whose stock has crashed may mechanically enter distress zone even if fundamentals are sound. Always cross-check with Piotroski F-Score and cash runway.
🔬 Case Diagnostic — Cyclical Steel Company at Trough
A steel company in a down-cycle: negative working capital from inventory write-downs, depressed EBIT, heavy debt load, cratering stock price.

5 Piotroski F-Score

9-Point Binary Fundamental Health Screen
🧠 Theoretical Concept

Joseph Piotroski (2000) created a 9-point binary scoring system to separate fundamentally strengthening companies from deteriorating ones. Each criterion scores 1 (positive signal) or 0 (negative/neutral). No partial credit.

The F-Score is particularly powerful as a long/short screen: go long high-F-Score value stocks (8–9), short low-F-Score value traps (0–3). Backtests showed significant alpha generation, particularly in small/mid-cap value universes.

📊 Factor Grid
Category Criterion +1 If...
Profitability Net Income ROA > 0 (positive net income)
Operating Cash Flow CFO > 0
ROA Change ROA increased YoY
Cash Flow Quality CFO > Net Income (accruals check)
Leverage & Structure Long-Term Debt Ratio LT Debt / Avg Assets decreased YoY
Current Ratio Current ratio increased YoY
Share Dilution No new shares issued in past year
Operating Efficiency Gross Margin Gross margin expanded YoY
Asset Turnover Asset turnover ratio increased YoY
📊 Thresholds
F-Score Signal Action
8 – 9 🟢 Strong Fundamental momentum. Long candidate in value screens.
4 – 7 🟡 Moderate Mixed signals. Requires deeper qualitative review.
0 – 3 🔴 Weak Fundamental deterioration. Value trap risk. Short candidate.
💡
strong>Cash Flow Quality Check (CFO > Net Income): This single criterion is one of the most powerful fraud and accruals detectors in the grid. If a company consistently reports net income significantly above operating cash flow, it is building accruals — revenue recognized before cash is collected. This is the accounting fingerprint of earnings manipulation (see Beneish M-Score below).

6 Beneish M-Score

Earnings Manipulation & Accounting Fraud Detection
🧠 Theoretical Concept

The Beneish M-Score is an 8-variable probit regression developed by Professor Messod Beneish (1999) to detect the probability that a firm is manipulating its reported earnings. It was famously used by Cornish and Titman students to identify Enron as a likely manipulator one year before its collapse.

The model does not prove fraud — it flags companies whose accounting patterns statistically resemble those of firms later caught manipulating financials.

📐 Mathematical Formula
Equation Formula
$$M = -4.84 + 0.920 \cdot DSRI + 0.528 \cdot GMI + 0.404 \cdot AQI + 0.892 \cdot SGI$$ $$+ 0.115 \cdot DEPI - 0.172 \cdot SGAI + 4.679 \cdot TATA - 0.327 \cdot LVGI$$
🔢 Variable Breakdown
Variable Formula What It Flags
DSRI $(Rec_t / Sales_t) \div (Rec_{t-1} / Sales_{t-1})$ Rising receivables relative to sales → revenue recognition gaming
GMI $Margin_{t-1} \div Margin_t$ Gross margin deterioration → pressure to manipulate
AQI $[1-(CA_t+PPE_t)/TA_t] \div [1-(CA_{t-1}+PPE_{t-1})/TA_{t-1}]$ Growth in intangibles/deferred costs → asset quality degradation
SGI $Sales_t \div Sales_{t-1}$ Rapid sales growth → incentive to sustain growth by any means
DEPI $DeprRate_{t-1} \div DeprRate_t$ Slowing depreciation → extending asset lives to boost earnings
SGAI $(SGA_t/Sales_t) \div (SGA_{t-1}/Sales_{t-1})$ Rising overhead relative to sales → efficiency deterioration
TATA $(NetIncome_t - CFO_t) \div TotalAssets_t$ The most powerful variable — total accruals. High = earnings not backed by cash
LVGI $Lev_t \div Lev_{t-1}$ Rising leverage → debt covenant pressure to inflate earnings
⚠️
strong>TATA is the nuclear variable. Its coefficient is 4.679 — by far the largest in the model. Total accruals (Net Income minus Cash from Operations, scaled by assets) directly measures the gap between accounting earnings and economic reality. Companies with persistently high TATA are reporting income they are not collecting in cash. This is the most reliable early-warning signal of earnings quality deterioration.
📊 Thresholds
M-Score Signal Interpretation
$M \leq -2.22$ 🟢 Very Low Risk Accounting appears clean
$-2.22 < M \leq -1.78$ 🟡 Gray Zone Monitor — some elevated indicators
$M > -1.78$ 🔴 High Manipulation Risk Accounting patterns statistically consistent with manipulation

7 5-Year Log-Linear Historical Regression Trend

Exponential Price Channels — Mapping Where a Stock Stands in Its Long-Run Vector
🧠 Theoretical Concept

Stock prices grow exponentially, not linearly. A stock that compounds at 20% annually does not add the same dollar amount each year — it adds an accelerating amount. Plotting raw prices against time and fitting a linear trend line systematically underestimates early-period prices and overestimates late-period prices.

The solution: Transform prices to log-scale. A log-linear regression on weekly data maps the constant percentage growth rate (CAGR) as a straight line. Standard deviation bands around this line create dynamic support/resistance channels calibrated to the stock's own historical volatility.

📐 Mathematical Formulas

Log transformation: $$y = \ln(Price), \quad x = \text{Weekly Trading Index (0, 1, 2, \ldots, 260)}$$

OLS Regression Coefficients: $$\beta = \frac{\sum (x - \bar{x})(y - \bar{y})}{\sum (x - \bar{x})^2}, \qquad \alpha = \bar{y} - \beta\bar{x}$$

Annualized CAGR from Slope: $$\text{CAGR} = e^{52\beta} - 1$$

Standard Error of Regression: $$\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{n - 2}}$$

Price Channel Bounds (±1.5σ): $$\text{Upper Band} = e^{(\alpha + \beta x + 1.5\sigma)}$$ $$\text{Lower Band} = e^{(\alpha + \beta x - 1.5\sigma)}$$

Current Price Deviation (Z-score from trend): $$Z_{deviation} = \frac{\ln(Price_{current}) - (\alpha + \beta x_{current})}{\sigma}$$

🔢 Variable Breakdown
Variable Definition Notes
$\alpha$ Intercept Log-price at week 0 of the regression window
$\beta$ Slope Log-price change per week. Multiply by 52 and exponentiate → CAGR
$\sigma$ Standard Error of Regression Volatility of log-price around trend line
$R^2$ Coefficient of Determination How well the trend line fits. >0.85 = strong trend, <0.5 = noisy/no trend
$Z_{deviation}$ Current Deviation from Trend Standard deviations above/below the 5-year regression midline
1.5σ Bands Channel Boundaries ±1.5 standard errors — empirically captures ~87% of price observations
📊 Diagnostics & Thresholds
$Z_{deviation}$ Signal Interpretation
$> +1.5\sigma$ 🔴 Overbought / Extended Price significantly above long-run trend. Mean reversion risk.
$+0.5\sigma$ to $+1.5\sigma$ 🟡 Elevated Momentum phase. Trend intact but stretched.
$-0.5\sigma$ to $+0.5\sigma$ 🟢 Fair Value Trading at or near historical trend. Neutral signal.
$-0.5\sigma$ to $-1.5\sigma$ 🟢 Discounted Below trend. Historically favorable entry zone.
$< -1.5\sigma$ 🟢🟢 Deep Value / Distress Far below trend. Either exceptional opportunity or broken trend.
⚠️
strong>The $R^2$ Gating Rule: Never interpret the channel bands for stocks with $R^2 < 0.60$. A low $R^2$ means the regression is not describing a real trend — it is fitting noise. High-volatility small-caps, biotech, and recent IPOs often fail this filter. The tool is designed for established companies with 5+ years of meaningful price history.
💡
strong>Why 1.5σ instead of 2σ? Standard normal 2σ captures 95% of observations. In log-price space, 1.5σ empirically captures ~87% of weekly closes for most large-caps — wide enough to filter noise, tight enough to generate actionable signals. Using 2σ produces bands so wide they lose diagnostic value.
